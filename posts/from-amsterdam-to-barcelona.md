# From Amsterdam to Barcelona

* cyrsullivan
* Oct 27, 2024
* 4 min read

After an uneventful flight from Ottawa, we touched down in Brussels mid September to launch our fall western Europe tour. We began in Maastricht, Netherlands, where we spent a few days touring with our good friends Paul Allen and his daughter Rachael. Maastricht is a surprisingly compact and dynamic town, well worth a visit. Another worthy stop is the nearby Abbey, Val Dieu. A Cistercian monastery turned brewery, it's well known for doling out spiritual refreshment.

Next, we ventured north by train to Zandvoort, Netherlands, a seaside town near Amsterdam. We whiled away the days strolling the city, Amsterdam and Harrlem (Sandy's grandmother’s hometown). Renting bicycles, we rode the breathtaking Dune Loop. A well-maintained bike path, it meanders through seaside dunes, lush forests and, unexpectedly, through Hillegom, Sandy's grandfather's birthplace. A perfect place to stop for a well deserved ice-cream.

Next, we set our sites on Maastricht collect Paul Allen, our unofficial tour guide, and begin our Battlefield Tour. Our target, Arras, France, a war-ravaged town close to the Western Front Line. Days were long and jammed packed, but extremely moving and thought provoking.  The must-sees sites were the Beaumont-Hamel Newfoundland Memorial, the Vimy Ridge National Historic Site and the Carrière Wellington (a fantastic, guided tour through the extensive tunnels under Arras). After an emotional four days we returned Paul to Maastricht, dropped Maureen and Roger at the Brussels Airport and hopped a train south to Lyon France.

Lyon is a vibrant cosmopolitan town with numerous UNESCO heritage sites. It's awash with stunning architecture and medieval charm. Paris-esque, it feels more approachable and relaxed. [It](http://relaxed.it)’s easily visited in a few days.  Again, we wandered the streets, visiting tourist attractions, sipping lattes and soaking in the atmosphere. We stopped by an ancient roman amphitheatre. Much to our amusement, it was hosting a Medieval Festival, sweet.

![](images/from-amsterdam-to-barcelona/image_17.jpg)

Avignon was our next stop. Recommended by Rick Steves - and brother Kevin, it was a last-minute addition to our itinerary. Avignon is home to a plethora of abandoned gems waiting to be explored. The town is centred around the Popes' Palace, a fortified palace of a colossal size. It was constructed by six successive Popes fleeing the unrest in Rome between 1309 to 1376 . We spent three days exploring the town before we continued south to Barcelona where we reconnected with Paul and Rachel Allen.

The colourful and trendy vibe of Barcelona was instantly palpable. We stayed in a chic pad on Passieg de Gràcia, a majestic boulevard that was a showcase for Barcelona's bourgeoisie at the turn of the 19th century. Time passes quickly when there's so much to see. In our short three day visit, we were able to explore the Basílica de la Sagrada Família and the Park Güell, and partake in a three hour guided walking tour of the ancient city centre. By pure happenstance, we were promenading the waterfront as one of the 37th America’s Cup races concluded. New Zealand won, oh what fun!

As much as we were enjoying Barcelona, it was time to pick up a rental car for a quick three day loop of the area. The objective, a pilgrimage to Montserrat, a walking tour in the micro country of Andorra, and a visit to the Dalí Theatre and Museum in Figueres. All three objectives were a hit and highly recommended for anyone in the area. The week ended with the gang returning to the Barcelona airport. For Paul and Rachel, it was homeward bound, for us it was off to Portugal!
